Introduction to Fractals. Viksec fractal, Newton fractal

ü§ì What is a fractal?

A fractal is a complex geometric shape that exhibits self-similarity at various scales. This means that as you zoom in on a small portion of the fractal, it appears similar to the overall shape. Fractals are generated through iterative mathematical equations, and they often have intricate and detailed patterns that repeat at different levels of magnification.

Key Characteristics of Fractals:

  1. Self-Similarity: Fractals display a pattern that repeats itself at different scales. This property is evident when you examine smaller and smaller portions of the fractal.
  2. Detail and Complexity: Fractals can be highly detailed and exhibit complex patterns. Even though the overall shape may seem simple, the details can be incredibly intricate.
  3. Non-Integer Dimension: Fractals often have a non-integer dimension, meaning that they don't fit neatly into standard Euclidean geometry. Instead, their dimension lies between whole numbers, reflecting their self-replicating and space-filling nature.

Fractals have applications in various fields, including mathematics, computer science, art, and nature. They are often used to model and understand natural phenomena, simulate realistic landscapes, and create visually captivating art. The Mandelbrot set is one of the most well-known examples of a fractal, and it has been extensively studied and explored in both mathematical and artistic contexts.

ü§ì Vicsek fractal

In mathematics the Vicsek fractal, also known as Vicsek snowflake or box fractal, is a fractal arising from a construction similar to that of the Sierpinski carpet, proposed by Tam√°s Vicsek. It has applications including as compact antennas, particularly in cellular phones.

Construction

The basic square is decomposed into nine smaller squares in the 3-by-3 grid. The four squares at the corners and the middle square are left, the other squares being removed. The process is repeated recursively for each of the five remaining subsquares. The Vicsek fractal is the set obtained at the limit of this procedure.

An alternative construction is to remove the four corner squares and leave the middle square and the squares above, below, left and right of it. The two constructions produce identical limiting curves, but one is rotated by 45 degrees with respect to the other.

ü§ì Newton fractal z^4 + C

The Newton fractal associated with the function f(z) = z^4 + C is a type of complex dynamic system and a variation of the more general Newton's method for finding roots of complex polynomials. In this case, z and C are complex numbers.

Here's a brief explanation of how the Newton fractal for z^4 + C is generated:

  1. Function Definition: The function f(z) = z^4 + C is used in the iterative process.
  2. Newton's Method Iteration: The Newton fractal is generated by applying Newton's method iteratively for each point in the complex plane. The iteration formula is given by: z(n + 1) = z(n) - f(z(n)) / f'(z(n)). The iteration continues until a certain condition is met or a maximum number of iterations is reached.
  3. Coloring Scheme: The resulting values of z after the iteration are often assigned colors based on certain criteria. For example, the number of iterations taken to converge to a root or a specified threshold might determine the color of a pixel.
  4. Visualization: The colored points are then used to generate an image, creating the visually striking and intricate patterns that characterize fractals. The areas of the fractal that take more iterations to converge are often associated with more intricate patterns.

The Newton fractal for z^4 + C can exhibit a variety of shapes and structures depending on the value of c chosen. Different values of c can lead to different patterns, and exploring the parameter space can reveal a rich diversity of visual outcomes.

Color schemes. CMYK, HSL

üé® What is a color model?

Color models are mathematical models that describe the way colors can be represented as tuples of numbers, providing a systematic way to express and interpret colors. These models are used in various fields, including computer graphics, imaging, and design. Different color models have different purposes and applications. Here are some commonly used color models:

üé® CMYK

CMYK is a subtractive color model used in color printing and describes the printing process itself. The name CMYK stands for Cyan, Magenta, Yellow, and Key (black), with "Key" representing the black color. In this model, colors are created by subtracting varying percentages of light from a white background.

Here are key points about the CMYK color model:

  1. Color Representation:
    • Cyan (C): Represents the color between blue and green.
    • Magenta (M): Represents the color between red and blue.
    • Yellow (Y): Represents the color between red and green.
    • Key (K): Represents the black color, which is added for depth and detail and to improve contrast. The use of "K" for black helps distinguish it from "B" used for blue in RGB.
  2. Color Mixing: In the CMYK model, colors are created by subtracting percentages of each ink color from white. For example, to produce red, one would subtract cyan, green would be produced by subtracting magenta, and blue by subtracting yellow.
  3. Printing Process: In color printing, CMYK is the standard color model. Printing devices, such as color printers and offset printing presses, use combinations of cyan, magenta, yellow, and black inks to reproduce a wide range of colors.

üé® HSL

HSL (Hue, Saturation, Lightness) is a cylindrical-coordinate representation of colors in a three-dimensional color model. It is designed to be more intuitive for human perception than some other color models, making it popular in graphic design, image editing, and other applications where users interact with colors.

Here are the key components of the HSL color model:

  1. Hue (H):
    • Description: Hue represents the type of color, often described as the dominant wavelength of light that is perceived.
    • Representation: Hue is typically represented as an angle around a color wheel, ranging from 0 to 360 degrees. The color wheel starts and ends with red, passing through yellow, green, cyan, blue, and magenta.
    • Example: A hue of 0¬∞ corresponds to red, 120¬∞ to green, and 240¬∞ to blue.
  2. Saturation (S):
    • Description: Saturation represents the intensity or vividness of a color. Higher saturation values result in more vibrant colors, while lower values lead to more muted or grayscale tones.
    • Representation: Saturation is typically represented as a percentage, ranging from 0% (gray or completely desaturated) to 100% (fully saturated).
  3. Lightness (L):
    • Description: Lightness represents the brightness of the color. It determines how much white or black is mixed with the color.
    • Representation: Lightness is typically represented as a percentage, ranging from 0% (black) to 100% (white). A lightness value of 50% corresponds to the "normal" color.

üìê What are affine transformations?

Affine transformations are a type of geometric transformation used in computer graphics, computer vision, and various other fields. They include a combination of linear transformations (such as rotation, scaling, and shearing) and translation (shifting). Affine transformations preserve straight lines and parallelism, but they don't necessarily preserve angles or distances.

The general form of a 2D affine transformation can be represented using a 3x3 matrix:

Here, the matrix elements a, b, d, e represent the scaling and rotation components, while c and f represent the translation components.

The transformation of a point (x,y) using an affine transformation matrix M is given by the following equations:

  • x' = ax + by + c
  • y' = dx + ey + f

In the context of computer graphics, affine transformations are commonly used to perform operations such as translation, rotation, scaling, and shearing on images or objects. They are efficient and widely used because they can be easily represented and manipulated using matrices, making them computationally efficient.

Applying these transformations to an object or an image allows you to achieve various visual effects and manipulate the position, orientation, and scale of the object.
